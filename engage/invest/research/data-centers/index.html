Context

Facilities teams are accountable for failures they can’t explain

In high-tolerance facilities, environmental conditions are assumed stable despite complex airflow, density, and thermal behavior. When failures occur, accountability sits with facilities, operations, and reliability teams—not an abstract “system.”

Point measurements aren’t explanations

Existing building management systems provide point measurements that detect threshold violations after damage occurs and do not explain why conditions drift or where instability originates.

Scale narrows tolerance

As facilities increase in scale, density, and utilization: environmental complexity increases while acceptable tolerance narrows. Blind spots translate into regulatory exposure, asset loss, or downtime.

Core Demand Drivers

  • Uptime + risk asymmetry: the downside of environmental failure vastly outweighs monitoring cost.
  • Asset density: high-density environments magnify the cost of small deviations.
  • Regulatory / reporting pressure: auditability, logging, and defensible narratives.
  • Energy constraints: HVAC optimization under cost/decarbonization constraints.
  • Operational scrutiny: “explain what happened” moments drive real buying.

TAM / SAM / SOM Logic

At-a-glance: TAM $70B → SAM $24B → SOM $3B. These are conservative, filtered to high-tolerance environments and exclude low-spec commercial buildings.

Addressable segment definition

  • Tight tolerances: small deviations cause outsized harm.
  • Continuous monitoring: persistent sensing + logging, not periodic checks.
  • High consequence of failure: downtime, regulatory exposure, yield loss, asset damage.
  • Operational sophistication: centralized BMS / specialized control infrastructure already exists.

Minimum viable market segment (MVMS)

A subset of operators is already forced to justify environmental stability continuously. Buying is triggered by audits, expansion/retrofit, or prior failures/near-misses.

SOM as an outcome

The obtainable segment is intentionally constrained to facilities where scrutiny and risk converge—where adoption is inevitable rather than aspirational.

Adoption & Buying Dynamics

Purchasing decisions are rarely exploratory. They’re initiated by audits/compliance, expansions/retrofits, commissioning deadlines, or prior deviation events.

  • Evaluation: reliability + auditability + integration with reporting workflows
  • Preference: explain behavior, not just detect violations
  • Timelines: driven by inspections and operational deadlines

Market Gaps & Structural Opportunity

Existing systems detect deviations but fail to explain environmental behavior. They’re optimized for threshold detection, local control loops, and compliance logging — not spatial variability, propagation, or causal origin.

  • Blind spots increase faster than sensor density as facilities scale
  • Point readings don’t survive “why was it stable?” scrutiny
  • Operators are accountable even when tools can’t justify the story

Labels: BMS = Typical BMS · Sensors = More sensors · Analytics = Analytics layer · Validity = Validity + co-timing

Conclusion

Environmental uncertainty is now an operational risk that cannot be deferred. Existing monitoring detects deviations but does not resolve uncertainty around cause, propagation, or spatial impact. A minimum viable segment exists where explanation is required and delaying action increases exposure.
