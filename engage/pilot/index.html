Pilots

Real sites. Real signals. Clear acceptance tests.

Each pilot is designed to answer one question: can we turn a drifting environment into a measurable field with receipts—not just nice plots.

What a Pilot Includes

  • Commissioning & acceptance tests (so results aren’t vibes)
  • Validity / trust layer shown in the portal (when we believe the measurement, and why)
  • Run receipts: device IDs, firmware/software versions, calibration state, configs
  • A pilot readout: findings, failure modes, and recommended next steps

Pilot Types

Pilot 1 — Cigar Lounge: 3D Temperature + Humidity Modeler

A short deployment that maps 3D humidity + temperature gradients across a cigar lounge (or humidor room) and turns them into a living model: where the room drifts, where pockets form, and what changes when doors open, HVAC cycles, or humidifiers kick.

What you get

  • 3D gradient map (zones + surfaces + “hot spots / dry spots”)
  • Dew point risk view (where/when condensation risk increases)
  • Timeline + event correlation (door opens, occupancy, humidifier cycles)
  • Trust indicators per time window (confident vs abstains)

Typical questions

  • Is the lounge actually stable, or just “fine near the thermostat”?
  • Do humidifiers overshoot locally and under-serve elsewhere?
  • Are we drifting at night / during HVAC cycles?
  • Where should sensors be placed so they reflect the true room?

Success criteria (examples)

  • Gradient model is stable and repeatable across comparable days
  • System flags when sensors are biased by placement (vents, dead zones)
  • Actionable recommendations: placement, humidifier tuning, airflow improvements

Pilot 2 — Data Center: 3D Temperature + Humidity Modeler

A deployment that builds a spatiotemporal model of temperature and humidity across aisles/zones (and where possible, rack-adjacent regions), emphasizing operational value: early warnings, commissioning validation, and evidence you can bring to stakeholders.

This is not “more sensors.” It’s better interpretation: alignment, validity checks, and receipts that explain why the model is trustworthy.

What you get

  • Zone-level gradient views (hot aisle/cold aisle behavior, return air patterns)
  • Humidity drift tracking (slow trends + sudden events)
  • Alarms with context (not just thresholds—why now, where, what changed)
  • Commissioning workflow: install → validate → baseline → go-live gate
  • Audit-ready receipts: versions, calibration state, validity uptime, change history

Typical questions

  • Are we seeing real environmental instability or sensor disagreement?
  • Which zones drift first when load shifts or containment leaks?
  • Can we detect risk earlier than existing BMS points?
  • What’s our “valid measurement uptime”—and what breaks it?

Success criteria (examples)

  • Reduced false alarms via validity gating and context
  • Faster root cause through timeline + spatial correlation
  • Clear commissioning “sign-off” that the measurement is trustworthy
  • Identified remediation targets (airflow tuning, placement fixes, containment issues)

Pilot 3 — Noise-Canceling “Air Bubble”

A pilot that demonstrates localized cancellation of a persistent nuisance (typically tonal or narrowband) by treating the environment as a timing problem: multiple pickups measure the nuisance, the system aligns them (co-timing), then cancels what’s truly shared—creating a practical “quiet bubble” region.

Think of it as a real-world validation of the core engine: align → verify validity → cancel → report what remains.

What you get

  • A demonstrated notch / reduction at target frequency bands in a defined area
  • Live diagnostics: coherence, drift, window validity (“safe to cancel” vs “do not trust”)
  • A failure-mode story: what breaks cancellation and how we detect it
  • A short engineering report: achieved reduction, stability over time, conditions required

Typical questions

  • Can you cancel this real nuisance, here, under real conditions?
  • How stable is cancellation when people move / doors open?
  • What sensor geometry and actuation placement is required?
  • What are the hard limits (multi-path, multiple sources, low coherence)?

Success criteria (examples)

  • Measurable reduction at target bands within the defined zone
  • System abstains (with reasons) when cancellation is unsafe/invalid
  • Clear recipe for scaling (or a clear “no-go” boundary)
