Why These Pilots Are Worth Doing

In commercial cannabis, “dashboard averages pass; pockets fail harvests” is not a slogan—it’s a default failure mode when dense canopies meet imperfect airflow and cycling dehumidification/HVAC.

  • Pockets inside the canopy are common. Crowding + poor air circulation is repeatedly cited as increasing local humidity and infection pressure—exactly the conditions that create “wet corners” even when the wall sensor looks fine. (See CSU Extension on powdery mildew being common in crowded plantings with poor air circulation and rising RH.)
  • Powdery mildew pressure is an industry staple. Guidance for hemp/cannabis-adjacent environments emphasizes humidity dynamics and airflow as key drivers for development and spread. (e-GRO powdery mildew of hemp.)
  • Botrytis (“bud rot”) can move fast under high RH. Peer-reviewed cannabis work reports rapid destruction of inflorescences under >70% RH with moderate temperatures—microclimates in flower can hit those conditions even if the room average doesn’t. (Botany / Canadian Science Publishing article on Botrytis on cannabis.)
  • Compliance failures (yeast/mold) and recalls are real. Reporting on major recalls cites batches exceeding mold and yeast limits; broader investigations describe recurring mold risk and inconsistent testing/oversight. (CPR recall reporting; WSJ investigation.)
  • Testing requirements vary by state (and can include Aspergillus targets). State microbial rules commonly require third-party lab testing; some jurisdictions explicitly require specific Aspergillus species. (Medicinal Genomics state-by-state microbial testing overview.)

What Every Pilot Includes

  • Commissioning & acceptance tests (so results aren’t vibes)
  • Validity / trust layer in the portal (when we believe the measurement, and why)
  • Run receipts: device IDs, firmware/software versions, calibration state, configs
  • Pilot readout: findings, failure modes, and recommended next steps

How We Scope Pilots (Why Tiers Exist)

Pricing scales with (1) scope of measurement geometry, (2) time horizon (cycles observed), (3) verification burden (proving a fix works), (4) deliverable sophistication (ops vs QA vs exec), and (5) stakeholder/support expectations (logins, handoffs, training).

The expensive part is verification

A one-time map is cheaper. Repeating scans across triggers (lights / irrigation / dehu cycles), running before/after intervention tests, and producing pass/fail evidence is where cost and value scale.

Canopy pockets are cycle-driven

Short pilots prove existence + location. Longer pilots capture repeatability, recurrence, and prevention performance—especially late flower and night control.
