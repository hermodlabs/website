Recurrence tracking + A/B tests + QA-grade evidence.

Why this exists (with references)

  • PM is widely documented as favored by humidity dynamics and poor circulation/overcrowding—recurring enough to be a staple problem.
  • Peer-reviewed cannabis work reports Botrytis can rapidly destroy inflorescences under high RH (e.g., >70%) and moderate temperatures.
  • Compliance failures tied to mold/yeast limits and large recalls have been reported; broader investigations describe ongoing mold risk in legal markets.
  • The difference vs a mapping pilot: Pilot D is structured for recurrence and proof—repeated capture across time, multiple A/B cycles, and explicit “safe to interpret” gating so conclusions remain defensible.

Deliverables (QA-grade)

  • Recurrence report: what repeats, where, and under which triggers
  • Risk-zone dashboard: pockets + persistence + change history
  • A/B efficacy package: before/after evidence with pass/fail criteria
  • Validity + abstains: explicit “safe to interpret/cancel” windows (and reasons when not)
  • Audit-ready receipts: versions, calibration state, configuration, and validity uptime

How to read the deliverables

Recurrence report

Use this to answer: Is it truly recurring—and what triggers it? Repeatable hotspot geometry under comparable triggers is the basis for prevention-grade claims (not “it happened once”).

Practical use: if recurrence only appears under a specific window (night control, irrigation transitions, dehu saturation), that window becomes the intervention test target.

Risk-zone dashboard

Use this to answer: What is the current risk state—and how has it changed over time? This is the operator + QA view: pockets, persistence, timing cues, and change history in one place.

Decision pattern: focus on pockets that are both persistent and severe; treat “one-off spikes” differently than “always-wet zones.”

A/B efficacy package (pass/fail)

Use this to answer: Did the intervention measurably reduce severity and/or persistence—with margin? The package is structured for a go/no-go decision, not a vibes-based “looks improved.”

QA framing: a “win” is measurable reduction in pocket severity/persistence in windows that are safe to interpret—and it survives re-checks.

Validity + abstains (safe-to-interpret)

Use this to answer: When are conclusions safe—and when should we abstain/cancel interpretation? Abstain is not failure; it’s the boundary that keeps evidence defensible under real-world drift.

Operator meaning: when measurement conditions aren’t trustworthy (coverage gaps, cycle mismatch, calibration stale), we don’t claim efficacy—even if the plot looks nicer.

Audit-ready receipts (provenance)

Use this to answer: What exactly ran, with what versions/config, and how much of the run was valid? This is what makes partner review, QA sign-off, and post-mortems possible without argument.

Practical use: if someone disputes results (“not the same setup”), receipts prove what changed and what did not.

Typical timeline (60–90 days)

Use this to answer: What happens when—and why does it take longer than a mapping pilot? Pilot D is designed to capture recurrence pressure and verify fixes across triggers and time (not one short window).

  1. Baseline: commissioning + initial zone map + pocket catalog
  2. Recurrence: repeated capture across cycles (especially night control and late flower)
  3. A/B cycles: multiple intervention tests (airflow, dehu strategy, placement fixes)
  4. Evidence: QA-grade writeups + pass/fail boundaries + “no-go” conditions

Acceptance tests (examples)

Use this to answer: What does “proof” mean here? These tests define the minimum bar for defensible prevention claims: recurrence, measurable efficacy, persistence across time, and abstain logic when conditions are unsafe.

  • Recurrence confirmed: pockets recur predictably under defined triggers—so you’re solving a real pattern, not noise.
  • Fix efficacy: intervention reduces pocket severity and/or persistence with measurable margin—pass/fail, not “seems better.”
  • Fix holds: efficacy persists across comparable cycles and time windows—so the fix survives real operations.
  • Abstain logic: system explicitly flags when measurement conditions make conclusions unsafe—preventing over-claiming.

  • A single remediation/downgrade event is financially brutal—prevention needs to be provable, not hoped for.
