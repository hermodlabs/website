Interview Pattern #1 — “The room looks fine until it doesn’t.”

Context

  • Multi-room facility. Mixed canopy density by room. Night control is “mostly stable.”
  • Standard wall sensors and a BMS. A few handheld spot checks. Lots of experience.

Struggle

  • Recurring PM pressure that doesn’t match the dashboard story.
  • A corner/zone “always feels different,” but nobody can prove it without camping in the room.
  • Leadership sees averages and asks why the team “can’t just hold setpoints.”

Desired outcomes

  • Find the pocket zones fast and stop treating the room as one number.
  • Catch the trigger windows (lights-off, night rise, dehu pinned).
  • Produce something leadership can read without an argument.

Constraints

  • Limited time for “science projects.” Must fit between turns and normal work.
  • Can’t disrupt SOPs too much; changes have cost and risk.
  • “We don’t need a fancy dashboard. We need clarity.”

Quotes (anonymized)

  • “If it’s not defensible, it doesn’t exist.”
  • “The numbers look fine. The canopy disagrees.”
  • “I don’t need ten charts. I need one answer: where is it failing?”

Implications for product

  • Zone map first: “bad zones” ranked with simple location anchors.
  • Trigger windows: lights-off / night / dehu pinned tagging.
  • Operator-proof output: a narrative report (“where/when/how often/how bad”).

Interview Pattern #2 — “Compliance isn’t a vibe. It’s a bill.”

Context

  • Operator supports production + QA. Testing requirements vary across SKUs/markets.
  • When a lot fails, the entire schedule bends around it.

Struggle

  • Failures feel “random” because measurement is coarse and cycle windows are missed.
  • Remediation + retesting burns labor and morale. Everyone becomes allergic to experimentation.
  • Hard to prove that changes reduced risk vs simply “got lucky this time.”

Desired outcomes

  • Reduce surprise failures by catching pockets before they compound.
  • Make interventions auditable: “we changed X, pocket behavior changed Y.”
  • Standardize a repeatable proof artifact for QA/leadership.

Constraints

  • Must not add heavy operational overhead.
  • Any new method must be simple enough for non-engineers to execute consistently.
  • Needs to survive the “prove it” conversation.

Quotes (anonymized)

  • “I’m not trying to be right. I’m trying to avoid a retest week.”
  • “If you can’t show repeatability, people call it luck.”
  • “Everything costs twice: once in product, once in schedule.”

Implications for product

  • Recurrence scoring: repeat vs one-off classification.
  • Before/after verification: intervention receipts + cycle-based comparisons.
  • Exportable evidence bundle: short narrative + key figures + timestamps.

Interview Pattern #3 — “Nights are where rooms tell the truth.”

Context

  • Late flower is the danger zone: dense canopy, blocked air paths, small issues compound fast.
  • Operators have a strong intuition that nights matter, but weak proof in the current instrumentation.

Struggle

  • Day readings look stable. Night windows are under-measured. Problems appear “out of nowhere.”
  • Dehu/HVAC staging can hide the moments when pockets spike.
  • Hard to justify cost/changes without a clear trigger story.

Desired outcomes

  • Capture the trigger windows automatically, not by camping in a room.
  • Make “late flower + nights” a measurable risk profile, not superstition.
  • Turn night behavior into actionable interventions: airflow paths, dehu strategy, staging, SOP timing.

Constraints

  • Night shifts are limited; observation capacity is low.
  • Any method must run unattended and summarize clearly.

Quotes (anonymized)

  • “If you want the truth, look at the night.”
  • “The room behaves when nobody’s watching.”
  • “I don’t need more sensors. I need the cycles.”

Implications for product

  • Cycle capture: lights on/off windows as first-class.
  • Event tagging: dehu pinned, HVAC stage shifts, irrigation windows.
  • Night summary: “worst zones at night” + duration-in-condition.

Synthesis: the three repeated demands

  • Location: “Where is it failing?”
  • Timing: “When does it trigger?”
  • Proof: “Does it repeat, and did the fix work?”

Anything that doesn’t answer those questions feels like “another dashboard.” Anything that does becomes operational leverage.
