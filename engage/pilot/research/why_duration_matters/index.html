Rooms don’t fail as a constant. They fail as a cycle. Lights on/off. Dehu staging. HVAC stages. Irrigation pulses. Transpiration waves. The canopy is a living, wet heat exchanger that amplifies small timing errors into big consequences.

That’s why duration matters: if you only measure the calm part of the day, you walk away with a clean story and a false sense of control. The worst pockets show up in the windows that are easy to miss — and those windows repeat.

Canopy pockets are cycle-driven

Pockets don’t usually sit there at the same intensity all day like a neon sign. They appear when the room gets pushed into a vulnerable phase: lights-off transitions, temperature drops, dew-point proximity, dehu pinned, airflow short-circuiting, post-irrigation moisture events, or the “nobody’s watching” hours at night.

When that happens, local zones go wet while the average stays polite. You can literally do the right thing all day and still lose the room in the same repeating window.

If you don’t catch the trigger window, the average looks “fine”

Averages erase timing. Timing is the whole crime. The question isn’t “what was RH?” — it’s: where did it spike, how long did it stay there, and did it repeat?

This is why operators end up trapped in the most insulting argument loop in cultivation: “We held setpoints.” / “Then why did it happen?” Both can be true — because the setpoint was true at the sensor, and the failure was true in the canopy. Product doesn’t grow at the sensor.

Short pilots vs long pilots

Short pilots = find + locate.
Short duration is for discovery: you map the room, locate the bad zones, and catch at least one representative cycle. You get a hit list of where to aim effort: corners, under-canopy lanes, behind racks, wall zones, return paths.

Long pilots = repeatability + recurrence.
Long duration is where you stop arguing and start proving: the same pockets show up again, under the same triggers, with the same timing. That turns “maybe” into “this is how the room behaves.” And once you can predict it, you can fix it — and verify the fix without guessing.

In JTBD terms, this is the difference between completing the job “find where the room lies” and completing the job “prove it repeats” + “verify the fix.” (See the mapping on the JTBD overview page.)

Best signal is late-flower + night cycles

Late flower is when the room stops being forgiving: biomass is up, bud structure is dense, and air paths are more likely to be blocked. Nights are where rooms reveal themselves: temperature drops, RH rises, and the canopy can quietly cross into “wet” without looking dramatic on a dashboard.

So if you want the best signal, you measure when the room is most likely to tell the truth: late flower and night windows. That’s where trigger windows show up, repeat, and prove they’re real.

What duration buys you (operator terms)

  • Less guessing: you’re not “feeling” the room — you’re measuring its repeating behavior.
  • Less arguing: “the pocket repeats at lights-off” beats “I think it’s a corner problem.”
  • Faster fixes: you aim interventions where they matter, not where it’s convenient.
  • Verification: you can prove a change reduced risk instead of hoping you got lucky.

That’s the point: duration isn’t a billing choice. It’s a feature requirement. If the system can’t see cycles, it can’t do the job operators hire it for.
