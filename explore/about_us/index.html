About HermodLabs

HermodLabs builds a co-timing engine: a platform for turning messy, drifting sensor streams into measurements you can trust.

Modern systems are full of signals that are “the same… almost.” Two microphones hearing the same hum. Two sensors sampling the same air. Two receivers watching the same oscillator. The plots look convincing until they don’t— deep notches one day, stubborn residuals the next.

We focus on the failure you can’t see in a screenshot: time. If two streams aren’t on the same clock, subtraction is not cancellation. It’s a gamble.

What the Co-Timing Engine Does

The co-timing engine is a repeatable recipe. We describe it as align, then cancel. The order matters. The honesty matters.

  1. Estimate relative delay on a trusted window

    We don’t assume the world is calm; we test for it. The engine works on windows where delay behaves like an honest step.

  2. Warp streams onto a common timebase

    Once streams share time, comparisons mean what you think they mean.

  3. Cancel what is truly shared

    If the nuisance is genuinely “the same up to delay,” it collapses. If it doesn’t, the residue is a diagnosis, not a mystery.

  4. Read what survives as an observable

    After cancellation, what remains can be tied back to geometry and medium—where the interesting physics lives.

A Platform, Not a One-Off Trick

We’re not shipping a single filter. We’re building an engine that can run on many benches.

The middle stays stable: delay bricks, windowed evaluation, diagnostics, and certificates that say when the assumptions hold. The benches change: audio rigs, humidity fields, RF timing networks, inertial sensors—anything that can present itself as:

  • shared-up-to-delay (on the window you claim), and
  • delay operators that behave predictably (enough to compose and test)

When a bench can make those promises, the same timing logic runs without being rewritten. When it can’t, the engine tells you which promise slipped.

Where We’re Headed

Our first wedge is medium-aware sensing: humidity and airflow conditions that quietly drift until they break a process, a product, or an SLA. The same timing structure that makes hum cancel (or fail) shows up in the harder problem: measuring an invisible field in 3D with enough trust to act on it.

We're building toward systems that can:

  • detect drift early,
  • isolate whether it's sensor behavior, timing, or the medium itself, and
  • turn “we think the air changed” into a measurement that can be audited.
