If you're considering working with us

If you're considering working with us, you deserve something better than glossy "culture" slogans.

This post is us putting our philosophy on the table-plain language, full transparency-because we want social proof that we mean what we say. We don't want you to "trust the user interface." We want you to understand the operating system.

Our north star: Asynchronous beats synchronous

Our biggest value is simple:

Asynchronous communication beats synchronous communication.

We didn't learn that from a leadership retreat. We learned it the boring way: computer science, distributed systems, parallel systems, and systems theory.

When you design a distributed system, you don't default to "everyone stop and join a call." You assume:

  • coordination is expensive,
  • latency exists,
  • people (and nodes) are doing real work,
  • and interrupt-driven architectures collapse under load.

So we apply the same principle to human work.

Synchronous communication is sometimes necessary. It is not the default.

Communication is not an asset. It's a cost.

This is where we depart from a lot of modern corporate culture.

We don't treat communication like an unlimited good. We treat it like compute and bandwidth.

Communication is a cost.

And like any serious system, we actively reduce costs that don't produce proportional value.

Because we've seen the failure mode-both in systems and in organizations:

  • too many meetings,
  • too many check-ins,
  • too many status updates that exist because nobody trusts the underlying process,
  • too much "alignment" that is really just anxiety management,
  • too many people blocked because the only way forward is to get time on someone's calendar.

Communication overhead is a major reason systems fail. That's not a hot take. That's the textbook.

So our cultural commitment is not "we communicate a lot."

It's we communicate intentionally.

What this looks like day-to-day

Here's what you should expect if you work here:

Default
What it means
1) Writing over meetings
We prefer a doc, a thread, a decision log, or a recorded walkthrough over a live meeting.
2) Default to clarity and receipts
If something matters, it gets written down. Decisions have context. Changes have reasons. You can catch up without asking someone to repeat themselves.
3) Fewer interrupts, deeper work
We protect long blocks of focus. We don't worship responsiveness. We worship progress.
4) Meetings are tools, not habits
If we meet live, it's because: we need a fast decision, we're unblocking something urgent, we're doing a high-bandwidth technical jam, or we're handling something sensitive that shouldn't live in text. Otherwise, we keep it async.
5) Trust the system, not the theater
You shouldn't need performative visibility to prove you're working. We build processes where the work speaks.

Engineering-first, not MBA-first

We are an engineering-first company.

That means:

  • we care about correctness and reality,
  • we like constraints and acceptance tests,
  • we respect time, focus, and craft,
  • we don't confuse activity for progress.

We are not MBAs.

(Except our COO-but we'll forgive him for that.)

Who thrives here

You'll probably love it here if you:

  • like writing and clear thinking,
  • prefer autonomy over constant coordination,
  • want fewer meetings and more real build time,
  • enjoy designing systems that scale (including the human ones),
  • want a culture that treats your attention as valuable.

You might not love it here if you:

  • need high-frequency verbal alignment to feel safe,
  • prefer decisions to happen in meetings instead of documents,
  • want constant social energy throughout the workday.

Neither is "better." But we're not going to pretend we're everything for everyone.

Why we're publishing this

Because we don't want culture to be marketing.

We want you to be able to evaluate us like an engineer would:

  • What do you optimize for?
  • What are your defaults?
  • What are your failure modes?
  • What tradeoffs are you willing to make?

This is ours.
