What was happening

When a room is truly uniform, adding sensors helps. But real cigar rooms are not uniform. They have pockets, boundaries, airflow paths, and slow drift. That is how you get more charts without getting more truth.

  • Two sensors disagree and nobody knows which one to trust.
  • The average looks fine while a local pocket quietly gets worse.
  • A fix improves one area and degrades another.
  • The dashboard stays calm until the room proves otherwise.

Why it is hard

Three realities collide in every serious room: it is spatial (pockets), temporal (drift), and interactive (controls push back). A single summary number cannot represent all three at once.

Local pockets

What matters is often the worst pocket, not the average.

Time drift

Drift is smooth, so it becomes normal before it is noticed.

Control feedback

Interventions reshape the field and can create oscillations.

What we are building

We are building a 3D humidity gradient modeler for The Cigar District. Instead of treating the room as one number, it produces a field map: where conditions are stable, where pockets form, and how the room changes under normal cycles.

What it enables for the room

Placement that makes sense

Store and rotate cigars by stable zones, not by a room average.

Earlier detection

Spot pockets early, before they persist long enough to matter.

Fewer probe arguments

Disagreement becomes explainable: different zones, not "bad sensors."

Targeted fixes

Act on the zone that is drifting instead of swinging the whole room.

How we know it is working

We judge success by operator-visible signals: a map that stays interpretable, disagreement that becomes attributable, and changes that can be verified in the zones that mattered.

Outcome
What you can observe
Stable vs. unstable zones
