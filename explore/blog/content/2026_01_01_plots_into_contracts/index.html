Why We Turn Plots Into Contracts (Heidegger, Gadamer, Ricoeur, Derrida)

A follow-up to “Religion Was Honest About the Problem”: four interpretive ideas that explain how belief gets manufactured by pipelines — and how we try to prevent it.

Jonathan Nacionales (Founder/CEO) • January 1, 2026

Last time: interpretation was the real problem

In the previous post, I argued something that annoys engineers on principle: a plot is a text.

Meaning doesn’t live inside the pixels. Meaning is what you get after you apply background assumptions: which window counts, which ruler is stable, what drift is allowed to do, what counts as “shared,” what counts as “success.”

If those rules stay implicit, the pipeline can become self-authorizing: the plot is used to justify the assumptions required to interpret the plot. That’s not measurement. That’s story manufacturing with a chart aesthetic.

This post is the sequel: why we treat the solution as contracts, certificates, and validity gates — and why a handful of philosophers accidentally gave us a clean way to describe the danger.

Hermeneutics is not a vibe — it’s the spec for “legitimate reading”

Hermeneutics is just the craft and theory of interpretation: the rules that make a reading legitimate. It started in texts (law, scripture, literature) because a text doesn’t interpret itself.

A bench plot is the same kind of object. If the interpretive rules are invisible, you can “read” the same plot into opposite conclusions and still claim the plot is on your side.

So the question becomes operational: what interpretive rules must hold for a plot to be allowed to make a claim about the world?

That’s the entire point of contracts, certificates, and validity gates. Not as philosophical decoration — as epistemic hygiene.

Four ideas that shaped how we think about “belief manufacturing”

Below is the compact version. Then we’ll unpack each one.

Thinker
Pipeline danger → Countermove
 Heidegger
Pipelines vanish on “good days,” hiding their premises → force the premises into the open as checks that can fail.
 Gadamer
Teams “read” the same plot through different horizons → standardize the horizon with explicit contracts.
 Ricoeur
Suspicion without a rebuild becomes cynicism → pair suspicion with restoration via receipts + certificates.
 Derrida
Authority leaks into loopholes → write contracts like security protocols: hostile-reader, anti-gaming design.

Heidegger: breakdown reveals the hidden toolchain

Heidegger’s useful idea is simple: when a tool works, it disappears. When it fails, the hidden scaffolding becomes visible.

On a “good day” bench run, the pipeline feels like reality. The notch is deep. The bars behave. The system becomes invisible precisely because it’s performing.

On a “bad day,” the pipeline becomes conspicuous. Cancellation smears. Windows look “haunted.” And suddenly you can see what you were living on:

  • the window wasn’t admissible,
  • delays didn’t commute,
  • the ruler drifted,
  • the “path” didn’t behave like the model you assumed.

That breakdown is not just annoying. It’s disclosure. It tells you the pipeline never gave you direct access to the world — it gave you access through promises.

Countermove: make those promises explicit and testable. If the pipeline depends on a calm window, say it and certify it. If it depends on delay+scale behavior, declare it and check it. If it depends on stable rulers, measure them.

Gadamer: understanding is a fusion of horizons

Gadamer’s warning is aimed at a fantasy engineers love: “apply the method and extract meaning.”

He argues understanding is not sterile extraction. We always interpret from within a horizon: training, language, expectations, and tradition. Those starting assumptions are not optional — they’re the equipment of reading.

Which explains a common bench phenomenon: two competent teams can stare at the same plot and disagree violently while both claiming to be “data-driven.” They didn’t see different pixels. They brought different horizons:

  • different definitions of “valid window,”
  • different priors about drift and stationarity,
  • different tolerances for ambiguity,
  • different meanings of “success.”

Countermove: standardize the horizon with explicit contracts. Don’t let “what counts” live in oral tradition. Write it down. Make it checkable. Make it fail loudly.

Ricoeur: suspicion is necessary — but restoration is the goal

Ricoeur names a modern habit: the hermeneutics of suspicion. After enough times being lied to by “obvious” interpretations, you learn to distrust the surface reading.

In engineering terms: suspicion is the refusal to let “it notched” be the end of the story. It notched, therefore it’s physics is not a proof — it’s a temptation.

But Ricoeur also warns that suspicion can harden into permanent cynicism. If suspicion is all you have, you don’t get truth — you get better ways to doubt everything.

So he pairs suspicion with restoration: rebuild meaning in a form that has earned the right to be trusted.

Countermove: certificates and receipts. Not vibes. Artifacts that turn an oral story into a public object:

  • what was assumed,
  • what was tested,
  • what passed,
  • what failed,
  • and why the interpretation is allowed (or not allowed).

That’s how suspicion becomes testable and restoration becomes legitimate.

Derrida: write your certificate like a hostile reader will exploit it

Derrida is useful here as a security posture. The moment you introduce a certificate, you create a new surface where belief can be manufactured.

If “valid window \(W\)” is operator-chosen, someone can window-shop until the rig has a lucky calm stretch and call it a property of the world.

If “deep notch” is the headline metric, someone can deepen the notch for the wrong reasons: over-flex a warp until “drift” becomes a broom, or tune preprocessing until the metric passes while the physics stays ambiguous.

Countermove: write the contract layer like a security protocol:

  • freeze how \(W\) is chosen (anti window-shopping),
  • bound admissible complexity (anti “drift broom”),
  • add decoy checks that fail when you’re merely subtracting,
  • make the easiest way to pass be the honest way: actually earning the interpretation.

Interpretation doesn’t vanish when you add rules. It relocates into the loopholes. So you close the loopholes.

So what are we trying to do, operationally?

We’re trying to prevent the plot from being both the evidence and the judge.

The contract stack is our operational definition of entitlement: interpretation is allowed only when the premises required to interpret the result have passed on the same window.

If entitlement fails, the conclusion is not “nothing happened.” The conclusion is: this pipeline cannot distinguish what happened.

So you don’t upgrade the plot into a claim. You reject the window, re-window, re-estimate, re-calibrate, log a reason code, and refuse to pretend.

Practical takeaway: treat your pipeline like a legal system

If interpretation is consequential, write down the rules that authorize interpretation.

  • Make them testable.
  • Make them fail loudly.
  • Make it harder to pass by story than by reality.

That’s not philosophy cosplay. That’s epistemic hygiene.
