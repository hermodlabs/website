The Job, Not the Dashboard

How we turn “humidity monitoring” into inventory positioning you can defend: start from the job, define the tasks, and ship receipts—not vibes.

Jonathan Nacionales (Founder/CEO) • January __, 2026
Back to Blog Read the previous post

A public product philosophy moment (but in a cigar shop)

A lot of software pitches start with features. Dashboards. Alerts. Integrations. “AI insights.” A parade of shiny nouns.

But in our pilot with The Cigar District, that approach fails immediately—because Bobby doesn’t run a museum of charts. He runs a room full of product that has to age consistently, sell consistently, and not surprise him two weeks later.

So we’re forcing ourselves to do the annoying thing first:

Start with the customer’s job. Work backwards.

The job statement (in Bobby-language)

If we’re being honest, the job is not “monitor humidity.”

That’s the trap.

The job is:

Help Bobby position inventory so cigars age well and stay consistent—without guesswork.

When you write it that way, a bunch of “obvious” features suddenly look optional. And a bunch of “boring” things become non-negotiable.

The tasks Bobby actually needs done

If the job is inventory positioning, here’s what Bobby needs—operationally—whether software exists or not:

  • Map the room into zones
    Stable zones vs unstable zones. Where the room behaves, and where it improvises.
  • Detect pockets and gradients
    The shelf that’s quietly different. The corner that drifts. The “room average” lie.
  • Place inventory deliberately
    Long-age goes here. Quick-turn goes there. Fragile wrappers don’t get sacrificed to the vent gods.
  • Tell real change from sensor drama
    Is this a real shift, or are two sensors arguing like interns?
  • Take a corrective action that isn’t random
    Move humidifier. Stage airflow. Relocate a shelf’s worth of boxes. Small, reversible actions first.
  • Verify the fix worked
    Not “it feels better.” A before/after receipt Bobby can understand.
  • Re-commission after changes
    Season changes. Layout changes. HVAC changes. The map must be refreshable, not a one-time art project.
  • Communicate rules to staff
    So the room doesn’t drift back into chaos because someone stocked “where there was space.”

Those are the tasks. Notice what’s missing: a dashboard as the center of the universe.

The workflow in one line

If you want the whole product in one sentence, it’s basically this:

Map → Place → Monitor → Diagnose → Intervene → Verify → Update rules

That’s the spine. Everything we build either supports this loop—or it’s feature gravity trying to add mass to the roadmap.

Receipts, not vibes: what “success” looks like

We translate each task into acceptance signals Bobby can observe.

Not KPIs that only make engineers happy. Receipts that an operator can audit.

Examples:

  • Stable zones actually behave (lower variance than unstable zones over the same period).
  • Pockets are repeatable (same shelf tends to misbehave, not random whack-a-mole).
  • Recommendations change outcomes (after an intervention, the zone stabilizes or the pocket shrinks).
  • Staff can follow the placement rules (without needing a 45-minute “training session,” which is just a meeting wearing a fake mustache).

If we can’t produce receipts, we’re not done. We’re just generating pretty telemetry.

The most important feature: “what do I do when you can’t decide?”

Real systems have uncertainty. The honest moment is when the system says:

“I don’t know.”

That’s not a failure. The failure is if “I don’t know” produces operational paralysis.

So we treat “can’t decide” as a first-class workflow branch:

  • Temporarily avoid stocking long-aging inventory in that zone
  • Run a short disturbance test and re-check
  • Collect another window of data
  • Add a temporary sensor for 48 hours
  • Re-commission the map

If we can’t tell Bobby what to do next, we don’t have a product—we have a research project.

What we intentionally do not build (yet)

This is the part that looks rude in a feature request meeting:

  • Not yet: integrations, exports, historical view mania
  • Not yet: “AI insights” that can’t produce receipts
  • Not yet: alerts for everything (alerts are where systems go to die)

We’ll build those later if they support the job loop. But we refuse to let the roadmap become a landfill of “good ideas.”

The takeaway

The hard part of building software isn’t building.

It’s deciding what you refuse to build so the product stays coherent.

If the job is inventory positioning, our output isn’t “humidity monitoring.” Our output is:

  • a zone map Bobby can use,
  • a pocket catalog he recognizes,
  • action recommendations that are doable,
  • and receipts that prove it worked.

Everything else is subordinate.

And yes: sometimes you have to not do things.
