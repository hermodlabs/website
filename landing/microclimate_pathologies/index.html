When the Air “Rings”

Imagine the air in a room as a big, invisible blanket. When something pushes that blanket upward (i.e wind over a hill, a supply jet hitting a rack, and a warm plume rising off canopy) the blanket lifts… then gravity (and buoyancy) pulls it back toward “level.”

It doesn’t just return and stop, though. It tends to overshoot, then correct, then overshoot again: up → down → up → down

Like bouncing on a trampoline—you don’t land perfectly still on the first contact. Those repeating “bounces,” moving through the air, are what we mean by gravity-wave–like oscillations.

Another way to picture it: flick a Slinky and you see a wiggle travel down its length. Air can do something similar: a vertical wiggle that propagates through the room.

The slightly more real explanation

Gravity-wave–like oscillations are waves in a fluid (air or water) that happen when the fluid gets pushed vertically and then tries to return to equilibrium. In a grow room, four ingredients make this show up:

Component Plain-language meaning Examples
Geometry The shape stuff
  • racks
  • walls
  • curtains
  • benches
  • canopy mass
  • duct placement
Airflow forcing The “push”
  • fans
  • HVAC supply/exhaust
  • dehumidifiers
  • door openings
Buoyancy The “hot air balloon” rule
  • warm air rises
  • cool air sinks
  • moisture plays too
Timing Repeating cycles
  • HVAC on/off
  • lights-off
  • irrigation
  • CO₂ events
  • door bursts

When you have obstacles + a vertical push + air that wants to stratify + repeating cycles, the room can start to sloshing back and forth—not randomly, but in patterns. That sloshing is the “gravity-wave–like oscillation.”

What actually creates the pockets

Think of the room as a 3D box where air is constantly being pushed around and constantly trying to “sort itself” by temperature and moisture.

1) Geometry creates channels and traps

Racks, walls, curtains, benches, canopy density, and duct placement form corridors, dead zones, and shadow regions. Like water swirling behind a rock in a stream, if a fan moves air “in general,” the flow can separate, curl, and stall behind obstacles.

2) Airflow forcing is the “push” that starts the motion

Fans, HVAC supply, exhaust, dehumidifiers, and door openings force air to move, but that forcing is rarely steady:

Each cycle is a repeating “kick,” like pushing a swing.

3) Buoyancy is the restoring force (the thing that pulls it back)

Buoyancy comes from density differences:

Condition Buoyancy outcome
Warm air is less dense Rises
Cool air is denser Sinks

Moisture complicates this further, especially near leaf surfaces and cold boundaries where condensation risk is higher. So when airflow lifts air up over an obstacle or into a corner, buoyancy (plus gravity) pulls it back, but it often overshoots. That overshoot is what turns “return to normal” into oscillation.

When the cycles repeat, you can get standing patterns: the same areas repeatedly go stagnant or wetter at the same point in the cycle.

Meanwhile a wall sensor can read “fine” because it’s sitting in a well-mixed zone—not inside the pocket that keeps re-forming. So “gravity-wave–like oscillations” is a fancy way of saying: Room layout + cycling airflow + buoyancy can create repeating, predictable pockets of bad conditions.

So what do you do with that? You diagnose it.

If gravity-wave–like oscillations create repeating pockets, then the room has something like microclimate symptoms:

Repeating symptom What repeats When it repeats
Wet corner The same corner gets wet At the same time every night
Still rack bay The same rack bay goes still After HVAC cycles
Condensing canopy layer The same canopy layer condenses Right after lights-off

The hard part is that those symptoms are easy to argue about. They show up as:

How it shows up Examples
A complaint
  • “it feels muggy back there”
  • “the air feels dead in that corner”
  • “it’s clammy under the benches”
A consequence
  • “we keep seeing mold in this lane”
  • “the same zone keeps failing QA”
  • “botrytis shows up in the same pocket every cycle”
A misleading average
  • “the room is 55% RH, we’re fine”
  • “VPD looks on target, so it can’t be humidity”
  • “average temp is stable—nothing’s wrong”

So we built DSM-MP — the Diagnostic & Statistical Manual for Microclimate Pathologies — as a way to turn “I think something is wrong” into a shared, testable claim. DSM-MP is a practical field guide built around one idea:

Principle Implication
If it repeats It has structure.
If it has structure You can name it.
If you can name it You can test it.

In practice, DSM-MP forces three clarifications before anyone touches a fan:

Diagnostic prompt How to think about it
What is repeating?
  • Fog layer after lights-off
  • Corridor plume after door events
  • Two-layer stratification pocket during HVAC cycles
What’s the likely driver?
  • Geometry trap
  • Cycling forcing
  • Buoyancy layer
  • Timing resonance
  • Or a combination stable enough to predict
What would count as “fixed”?
  • Not “the room feels better.”
  • This pocket no longer forms in the defined window after the trigger.

That’s what DSM-MP turns “weird room vibes” into:

Output What it means
Diagnosis What pattern is repeating.
Mechanism hypothesis Why it repeats.
Testable fix One knob, comparable cycles, receipts.

DSM-MP has 4 axes (and 16 types)

Once you can name a repeating pathology, the next question is: why is it still allowed to exist? In practice, rooms don’t fail for one reason. They fail because one or more core capabilities are missing. DSM-MP organizes that into four axes:

Axis Name Plain-language test What it’s not
O Observability
  • Can you see the truth at the canopy—where the argument actually lives?
  • Not “a wall sensor,” but placement, cadence, and enough context to trust what you’re seeing.
 “We have a wall sensor.”
M Moisture control
  • Can you reliably clear moisture debt after triggers (lights-off, irrigation, load spikes)?
  • Not just “dehu runs,” but does the risk actually go away in the pocket?
 “The dehu runs.”
A Air movement
  • Does air exchange through canopy depth—or does it stall into repeatable dead zones?
  • Not “fans exist,” but mixing where it matters.
 “We have fans.”
P Proof loop
  • Can you run changes like tests: one knob, comparable cycles, and receipts?
  • Not “we tried stuff,” but we can prove or abstain.
 “We tried stuff.”

The 16 classifications (type codes)

Each axis can be either:

  • present (solid enough to rely on), or
  • missing (too weak to support decisions)
That creates 16 DSM-MP type codes as a compact way to describe what a room can and can’t do yet. Think of it like diagnostic shorthand:

Room profile What happens
A room with strong O + weak P Can see problems but can’t verify fixes (endless tweaks).
A room with weak O + weak A May be fine on averages but produces “mystery mold” pockets.
A room with strong P Can improve fast—because it can run honest experiments.

A simple way to read the grid

Here’s the legend that makes the grid intuitive:

Axis What it answers
O Do we actually know what’s happening?
M Can we clear moisture risk on schedule?
A Can we move air through the canopy where pockets form?
P Can we prove a change worked (or abstain honestly)?

DSM-MP isn’t a score to brag about. It’s a way to choose the first move without wasting a month. Rule of thumb: the weakest axis limits what you’re allowed to claim.

If this axis is weak… Then…
P is weak You can’t declare “fixed.”
O is weak You can’t even declare “found.”
A is weak Pockets will keep re-forming no matter what the average says.
M is weak The room can look stable until a trigger pushes it over the edge.

How it connects back to the pathologies

The mold patterns you see (i.e lights-off fog bank, door corridor plume, and HVAC stratification pocket) are the symptoms. DSM-MP type codes tell you what kind of facility you’re working with and what to do next:

Missing axis What you need first
O is missing You need better placement/cadence before you “debate the room.”
A is missing You need mixing/jet control where the pocket forms.
M is missing You need moisture-debt control tied to triggers.
P is missing You need the proof loop so fixes don’t become folklore.

A few DSM-MP patterns you can spot quickly

Once you’ve got the DSM-MP lens (i.e. pockets + triggers + repeatability) the next step is to start naming what you’re seeing. Below are three common “day one” pathologies: they recur, they have a signature, and they point to a first test.

1) Lights-Off Fog Bank

Lights-off is a fast regime change: leaf temperature shifts, evap/condensation dynamics move, and air that was “fine” can suddenly cross a dew-point threshold.

Section Details
Signature
  • a thin humidity/condensation layer that shows up reliably after lights-off
  • wetness that appears in the same zones without an obvious “spill” event
Where it hides
  • inside canopy depth
  • under benches / behind racks
  • near cold boundaries (walls, coils, supply ducts)
Why the wall sensor stays calm
  • it sits in a well-mixed zone
  • the failure is a layer, not a room-wide average

2) Door-Corridor Plume

A door opening is a pressure and exchange event. It can inject a narrow plume of different temperature/moisture that travels down an aisle, then curls and stalls when it hits geometry.

Section Details
Signature
  • a corridor-shaped “lane” of off-spec air that repeats with door traffic
  • localized problems that track an aisle more than a whole room
Where it hides
  • along a single corridor or rack lane
  • in the wake behind racks and curtains
  • where the plume hits its first major obstruction
Why averages look fine
  • it’s intense but narrow
  • the room stays “normal” while one lane gets hammered

3) HVAC Stratification Pocket

HVAC can accidentally create a two-story room: supply jets and returns establish layers, and mixing may be weaker than it feels at the wall.

Section Details
Signature
  • stacked conditions: one layer warmer/drier, another cooler/wetter
  • pockets that show up at the same phase of the HVAC cycle
Where it hides
  • upper vs lower canopy layers
  • under supply jets or between jet paths
  • behind dense canopy where mixing collapses
Why the dashboard misses it
  • sensors can sit in the “good layer”
  • the failure is vertical structure, not a single number

The point of naming them

When a pattern has a name, it stops being a debate and becomes a test:

Trigger What to look for
Lights-off Look for a repeatable fog layer in a defined window.
Door events Look for a corridor plume and its stall point.
HVAC cycling Look for persistent stratification and pocket layers.

The UI is the countermeasure

A big reason microclimate problems survive is that it’s a process:

Step What happens What it looks like on the floor Why it doesn’t resolve the root cause
1 People notice a wet corner. “That spot is clammy again” / recurring sheen, condensation, or leaf wetness in the same zone It’s an observation without a named pattern window (when it forms, where it peaks, what triggers it)
2 Someone adjusts a fan. A quick tweak: angle change, speed bump, relocation—done to “hit the spot” Multiple knobs move at once; no controlled comparison, so you can’t tell if the pocket actually stopped forming
3 The wall sensor looks fine. Dashboard reads “in spec,” so urgency drops and the team moves on Wall sensors often sit in well-mixed air; localized layers/pockets can fail while the room average stays green

A big reason microclimate problems survive is that it’s process: A week later the mold comes back… and the team argues about what changed. So we built the UI as a countermeasure to the two things that kill real fixes:

Failure mode What it means
Drift The claim quietly changes over time.
False green The dashboard looks fine while the pocket is still failing.

DSM-MP gives you names for the pathologies and the UI makes those names operational:

  • measurable,
  • repeatable, and
  • verifiable.

1) “Freeze the claim” so it can’t morph into a meeting

Every investigation starts by locking a slice of reality into a clear handle:

Handle What you lock down Why it matters Example
Where (zone) The exact location of the pocket / symptom Turns “somewhere back there” into a map-able target you can revisit and compare “Rack B, Bay 3, lower canopy, aisle-side corner”
When (trigger + window) The event that initiates it and the time window to watch Prevents arguments by making the claim falsifiable: it either forms in the window or it doesn’t “Lights-off + 0–45 min” or “Door burst + 0–10 min”
What context (stage / setup) The operating conditions that make the observation comparable Stops “it depends” drift—if the stage/setup changes, you’re not testing the same system “Week 5 flower; curtains deployed; dehu setpoint 50%; fans at 60%”

This is how you stop the classic failure mode:

Classic failure mode What it signals What DSM-MP forces you to specify
“We fixed humidity.” A broad claim with no named pocket or measurement context Which metric, where measured, and what “in-spec” means in the failing zone
“No, we fixed airflow.” Competing narratives because multiple knobs changed (and no controlled test) The single change made, plus what you expected it to change in the pocket
“Wait, which room?” Scope confusion—people are talking about different systems Site / room identifier and any relevant geometry or equipment differences
“Which night?” Timing drift—different conditions, different cycle, different outcome The trigger and the observation window (and which cycle you’re comparing to)
“Which rack?” Spatial drift—localized pockets get averaged away when location is vague Exact zone: rack/bay/height (and whether it’s aisle-side, wall-side, etc.)

The UI forces a single sentence you can’t wiggle out of: “We are talking about this zone, in this window, after this trigger, in this stage.” That sentence becomes the anchor for everything that follows.

2) Baseline first: capture the room as-is (the “crime scene”)

Before you change anything, the UI treats the current state as evidence. Not “data logging,” but baseline capture:

Baseline capture item What you record Why it matters Example
What the room was doing The observed behavior + any obvious pocket signatures Creates a “before” that future comparisons can actually anchor to “Wet corner forms after lights-off; clears by +60 min; only in Rack B lower canopy”
What cycles were active The repeating programs running during the observation Lets you line up comparable cycles instead of comparing different operating modes “HVAC 10/10 min; dehu setpoint 50%; lights 12/12; CO₂ pulse hourly”
What sensors were where (even roughly) Sensor placement map / rough coordinates and height Prevents false-green arguments by showing which readings were in well-mixed zones vs pockets “Wall sensor at 5 ft near door; probe in canopy at Rack B Bay 3 lower; one sensor near supply jet”
What you think the trigger is The suspected initiating event + expected window Makes the claim testable: if the trigger fires, the pocket should appear (or not) “Lights-off → 0–45 min window” or “Door burst → 0–10 min window”

This matters because most teams skip it and later try to reconstruct reality from memory. Baseline is your ruler.

3) Time becomes legible: events turn into rulers

Rooms don’t fail “at 2:13am.” They fail relative to triggers:

Trigger-relative failure What the trigger changes What to watch for Typical window
After lights-off Heat input drops; leaf/surface temps shift; RH rises toward saturation in pockets Fog/condensation layer, leaf wetness, sudden RH/VPD divergence between zones Minutes to ~1 hour after event (repeatable if it’s real)
After HVAC ramps Airflow pattern changes; jets turn on/off; mixing and stratification reconfigure Layering (warm/dry vs cool/wet), “still” bays, pocket formation at same cycle phase Within a few minutes of ramp; can persist through the cycle
After a door burst Exchange pulse + wake/vortex transport; corridor air injected along a lane Corridor plume, aisle-shaped off-spec “lane,” first-obstruction hit point Immediate to ~10–15 minutes (depends on traffic and ventilation)
After irrigation rebound Moisture load increases; evap/transpiration shifts; latent load rises Localized RH spikes, slow dry-down, recurring wet zones where debt doesn’t clear Tens of minutes to hours (until moisture debt is cleared)

The UI makes those triggers first-class objects, so you can look at conditions aligned to events, not just a wandering line chart. That’s the difference between “noise over time” and “a repeating pattern.”

4) Pockets become objects you can point to (not feelings)

Instead of “it’s wet back there,” the UI creates an evidence object:

Evidence object field What you capture Why it matters Example
Location (rack/height) The exact zone where the pocket forms (and its vertical position) Prevents “somewhere back there” drift; makes the claim revisit-able and mappable “Rack B, Bay 3, lower canopy (18–24 in), wall-side”
Trigger association (what sets it off) The initiating event and the observation window Makes the symptom testable: trigger happens → pocket should appear (or not) “Lights-off → forms within 0–45 min”
Persistence (how long it lasts) How long the pocket remains off-spec before clearing Separates brief transients from sustained risk (and tells you what “fixed” means) “Persists ~70 minutes; clears by +90 min”

Now you can rank, walk, and re-check the exact same thing. This is DSM-MP in practice: a named pathology becomes a trackable entity.

5) Gates prevent the system from “pretending green”

Real facilities are messy:

Real-world mess What it causes How DSM-MP/UI compensates
Sensors get bumped Readings become hard to compare across days; “same sensor” isn’t the same context Record a rough placement + height each time and treat moves as a version change in the evidence
Placement is uncertain False-green risk: sensors may sit in well-mixed air while pockets fail elsewhere Use zone labels (rack/bay/height) and confidence notes (“approx”) instead of pretending it’s exact
Events aren’t logged You can’t align cycles; people argue about what “caused” the pocket Anchor observations to trigger-relative windows (“after lights-off + 0–45 min”), even when timestamps are imperfect
A fan was changed “a little” Multiple knobs shift silently; outcomes look random and fixes become folklore Force “one-knob” change notes (what moved, how much, where) and require comparable cycles before declaring improvement

Most software silently averages through that mess. Our UI does the opposite. It gives you explicit gates like:

UI gate What it asks you to decide Why it matters Example
Known vs unknown Do we actually know this—or are we assuming? Prevents “confident nonsense” from becoming operational truth “Door burst time is unknown” (don’t treat it as a clean trigger)
Comparable vs not comparable Are we comparing the same operating conditions and cycle phase? Stops false conclusions from apples-to-oranges days “Before was veg + curtains open; after is flower + curtains closed” → not comparable
Trusted vs uncertain How confident are we in sensor placement/quality and the measurement context? Makes “false green” visible by separating reliable evidence from shaky readings “Sensor bumped yesterday; placement approximate” → uncertain evidence

If something is unknowable, the correct output is not a pretty chart. It’s ABSTAIN. That’s not pessimism. It’s integrity. It prevents false confidence.

6) One knob at a time: fixes become experiments

The UI nudges you toward a simple discipline: Make one change. Re-run. Compare. Verdict. Because if you change five things, you didn’t fix anything. You just changed the story. So the workflow is designed around:

Workflow step What you do Why it matters Output you should get
Baseline (ruler) Capture the current behavior and the pocket signature under normal operation Gives you a stable reference so you’re not debating memory A “before” record: zone + trigger + window + severity/persistence
Intervention (one knob) Change one thing you believe should affect the pocket Keeps causality interpretable; avoids story inflation from multiple changes A single, explicit change note (what/where/how much)
Verification run (same ruler) Re-run comparable cycles and observe the same window with the same measurement context Makes “after” evidence comparable instead of opportunistic An “after” record collected with the same criteria
Comparison (did the pocket actually die?) Compare pocket formation, severity, and persistence against baseline Forces a real verdict: improved, unchanged, worse, or uncertain A verdict you can defend (and repeat), not “it felt better”

7) The verdict is the product

The final output is not “a dashboard.” It’s a decision you can stand behind:

Decision question What “yes” looks like How you verify
Did the lights-off fog bank disappear in the defined window? The layer no longer forms (or is materially weaker/shorter) after lights-off across comparable cycles Check the same zone at the same post–lights-off window; compare to baseline severity/persistence
Did the door corridor plume stop reaching canopy depth? The corridor “lane” no longer penetrates to canopy depth, or it decays before the problematic rack/bay Observe immediately after door events; compare plume penetration/decay and affected-lane signature vs baseline

And if comparability fails, the UI doesn’t let you declare victory just because the line “looks better.” It forces the honest answer: we don’t know yet.
